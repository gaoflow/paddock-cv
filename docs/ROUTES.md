# Routes In — how people reach the F1 race-engineer seat (2026 grid evidence)

> Findings distilled from the private dataset: the CVs of the 26 named
> race-engineering staff across all 11 teams on the 2026 F1 grid,
> reconstructed from public sources and confidence-graded. The per-person
> roster stays in the private dataset (see the README data notice); what
> follows are the aggregate patterns, which is the part you can act on.

## What the race-engineering CVs tell you

**Two dominant doors dominate the sample:**

- **`uni → junior single-seaters / in-house` (15 of 26).** Engineering degree →
  trackside in F3/F2/GP2 or sportscars, *or* hired young into an F1 team and
  promoted up through design/simulator/performance roles. The purest in-house
  version: both McLaren race engineers went Cambridge → McLaren and never left.
- **`data / systems engineer → RE` (8 of 26).** Join an F1 team directly as a
  data, systems or controls engineer and climb data → **performance
  engineer** → race engineer.
- **`motorsport MSc → F1` (2 of 26).** A specialist motorsport MSc can be the
  accelerator when paired with early F1 performance or design experience.

**The climb is real and slow.** Median **8 years** from first F1 job to first
race-engineer seat (range 0–17). The radio is *earned from inside*; it is
almost never a direct hire. Every "0–3 year" case in the sample had already
done their race-engineering apprenticeship in junior categories or at another
F1 team first.

**Performance engineer is the launchpad.** Nearly every RE was a *performance
engineer* immediately before getting the seat. If you want the radio, that's
the job to target first.

**The degree is the ticket.** Every engineer with a documented education holds
an MEng/MSc in mechanical, aerospace or automotive engineering. Recurring
schools: **Cambridge, Sheffield, Oxford Brookes (motorsport MSc),
Loughborough, Cranfield, Strathclyde, Imperial, Politecnico di Milano,
ESTACA**.

**Adjacent roles below/around RE:** the repeatable ladder is usually
data/systems/controls → performance engineer → race engineer. Other important
trackside roles include PU performance/systems engineer, tyre engineer,
trackside aerodynamicist, strategy engineer/strategist and
simulation/simulator engineer — see the **Role Map** tab of the dashboard.

## The playbook (if "race engineer" is the seat you want)

1. **Degree** — MEng/MSc mechanical / aero / automotive. A specialist
   *motorsport-engineering MSc* (Oxford Brookes, Cranfield, Southampton) is a
   proven accelerant in the sample.
2. **Get in any garage** — two doors: (a) trackside in junior single-seaters
   or sportscars/WEC, then jump to F1; or (b) join an F1 team directly as a
   **data / systems / controls** engineer (the most common true entry role).
3. **Climb to performance engineer** — data/systems → **performance
   engineer**. This is the proving ground; do it well next to a race driver.
4. **Wait for the seat** — ~8 years of trust-building; a seat opens when a
   senior RE is promoted upward and the performance engineer behind them
   moves up.
5. **Have an edge** — aero/vehicle-dynamics depth, an OEM academy, or a
   standout adjacent record (elite sport, unusual series experience) each
   recur as differentiators in the sample.

## Public case studies worth reading

Two arcs that are extensively documented in mainstream F1 press coverage:

- **Peter "Bono" Bonnington** — Jordan data engineer → Brawn performance
  engineer → race engineer; the most successful driver-engineer pairing in
  history (six titles with Hamilton). The canonical `data-eng → RE` arc.
- **Laura Müller** — first female full-time F1 race engineer (Haas);
  TU Munich → sportscars/DTM → simulator → performance engineer → race
  engineer in three years inside the team.
